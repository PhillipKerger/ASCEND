Multiagent research protocol

Use [NAME OF THE AVAILABLE MULTIAGENT SYSTEM] aggressively and dynamically. Up to
[MAXIMUM CONCURRENT AGENT COUNT] agents may be used.

Do not begin with a fixed allocation such as “assign a predetermined number of agents to
each named approach.” Instead, manage the research adaptively according to the quality,
novelty, and rigor of the mathematical progress being returned.

Begin with a genuinely diverse portfolio of approaches. Agents should investigate
substantially different mathematical mechanisms, selected and adapted to the problem.
Possible families include:

- direct constructions or direct proofs;

- induction on a natural complexity measure;

- minimal-counterexample and extremal arguments;

- structural decompositions and local-to-global principles;

- invariant, potential-function, or charging arguments;

- algebraic, analytic, geometric, topological, combinatorial, logical, or

- probabilistic methods, random restrictions, concentration, coupling, or derandomization;

  circular;

- constructive and nonconstructive routes pursued independently;

- explicit search for counterexamples to proposed intermediate claims;

  small obstructions;

- formal barrier results for carefully defined families of methods;

- [INSERT APPROACH FAMILIES PARTICULARLY NATURAL IN THE RELEVANT FIELD].

Do not tell most agents the currently favored approach during the initial rounds. Preserve
independence so that agents do not all converge on the same attractive but incomplete

Maintain an explicit registry of approach families. Group agents according to their actual

For every approach family, record:

- the principal idea or representation;

- the exact theorem, lemma, construction, or obstruction being pursued;

- the quantity, invariant, or certificate on which the route depends;

- the assumptions currently being used;

- the strongest rigorously proved result obtained by the route;

- dependencies on other active routes;

- whether the route is active, blocked, refuted, superseded, or ready for audit.

If too many agents converge on one family, redirect some of them toward underexplored
formulations.

Do not allow an approach to dominate merely because it produces an elegant reduction. A
route ending at a lemma equivalent in strength to the original problem is not close to
completion unless it supplies a genuinely new proof of that lemma.

When an approach stalls at a theorem-strength missing statement, mark it as blocked.
Continue assigning agents to it only when someone proposes a materially new invariant,
construction, decomposition, estimate, induction parameter, or transfer mechanism.
Renaming or restating the same missing global claim does not count as a new mechanism.

Keep several incompatible proof routes alive through multiple rounds. Cross-pollinate ideas
only after independent agents have developed them far enough to expose their genuine
strengths, hidden assumptions, and exact failure points.

The root agent must repeatedly:

1. synthesize the strongest rigorous findings;

2. identify common hidden assumptions or recurring obstacles;

3. challenge the most promising claims;

4. redirect resources away from duplicated or circular approaches;

5. launch new agents on newly exposed subproblems;

6. commission counterexample searches against proposed lemmas;

7. combine ideas only when the compatibility conditions are formally proved;

8. reopen blocked routes only when a genuinely new mechanism has appeared.

Do not stop after the first wave of agents fails.

Computational exploration

Use computational agents strategically when finite experiments can clarify the theory.
They may:

- enumerate small instances or low-complexity objects;

- search for counterexamples to proposed lemmas;

- compare exact solutions with relaxations or heuristics;

- inspect extremal configurations;

- test candidate invariants;

- symbolically verify identities in representative cases;

- identify the smallest failure of an overstrong conjecture;

- generate examples distinguishing two competing formulations;

- suggest sharper constants or missing hypotheses.

Every computational task must have a stated mathematical purpose.

Computations may guide conjecture formation, falsify false lemmas, and check proof

first been rigorously reduced to a finite computation and the completeness of that
reduction is proved.

Adversarial auditing requirements

Use dedicated adversarial agents throughout the search.

Before launching the main research process, add a problem-specific audit list containing
the subtle failure modes most likely to invalidate a proof in this area. These items should
be as concrete as the definitions in the task statement.

At a minimum, every proposed solution must be audited for the following.

1. Exact target statement

Check that the proof establishes the theorem with the original quantifiers, hypotheses,
conclusion, and parameter dependencies. Verify that it has not proved a nearby but weaker
statement.

2. Definitions and conventions

Check every use of the principal definitions. Verify all empty, trivial, singular,
degenerate, boundary, zero-valued, or infinite cases specified in the task statement.

3. Hidden assumptions

Search for unannounced assumptions such as genericity, compactness, smoothness,
integrality, boundedness, finiteness, independence, uniqueness, nondegeneracy, regularity,
uniformity, or access to additional structure.

4. Preservation under transformations

For every reduction, normalization, quotient, completion, embedding, perturbation,
contraction, extension, relaxation, discretization, limiting operation, or change of
representation, verify that:

- the transformed object is well defined;

- all required hypotheses are preserved;

- solutions can be transferred in the required direction;

- the target quantity changes by a controlled amount;

- no information needed for reconstruction is lost.

5. Local-to-global reasoning

Check that locally valid statements actually combine into the required global conclusion.
Demand a proof of every compatibility, gluing, consistency, extension, or reconstruction
claim.

6. Use of external theorems

Verify the exact statement and hypotheses of every cited theorem. Check that the theorem
applies to the permitted object class, parameter range, regularity level, computational
model, and notion of solution.

7. Circularity and equivalent restatements

Reject any argument that assumes a statement equivalent to the target, hides the target
inside a definition, or transfers the entire difficulty into an unproved master lemma.

8. Quantitative bookkeeping

Check every inequality, constant, asymptotic term, normalization, error estimate, and
parameter substitution. Verify that individually small errors remain small after all
iterations, levels, regions, or recursive calls are combined.

9. Limiting and infinitary arguments

Where limits, compactness, convergence, infinite sums, measure-theoretic operations, or
choice principles occur, verify:

- the required compactness or completeness;

- the mode of convergence;

- interchange of limits and other operations;

- preservation of the desired property in the limit;

- effectiveness, if the target is constructive.

10. Probabilistic reasoning

Check independence assumptions, conditioning, measurability, failure probabilities,
expectations, tail bounds, union bounds, and derandomization. An expected bound does not
automatically yield one outcome satisfying all required properties simultaneously.

11. Optimization and approximation

Where relevant, verify:

- that the benchmark is correctly defined;

- that every relaxation is a valid lower or upper bound in the needed direction;

- that fractional or relaxed objects can be converted to admissible objects;

- that the actual cost or objective of the constructed object is bounded, not merely a
  surrogate quantity;

- that additive errors can genuinely be absorbed into the required multiplicative or
  asymptotic guarantee;

- that the zero-benchmark case is treated separately when necessary.

12. Algorithmic correctness

Where relevant, verify:

- that every step is computable in the stated model;

- that all subroutines have the claimed guarantees;

- that the algorithm terminates;

- that the returned object is valid;

- that correctness and approximation guarantees apply to the same execution or event.

13. Complexity and encoding

Where relevant, check:

- the dependence of the exponent on every parameter;

- hidden pseudo-polynomial dependence;

- numerical precision and rational arithmetic;

- growth of intermediate representations;

- oracle assumptions;

- uniformity;

- preprocessing and reconstruction costs;

- whether the claimed input-size measure includes the complete encoding length.

14. Constructive versus existential claims

Check whether the target requires an explicit or efficiently computable witness. An
existence proof does not imply an algorithm unless the extraction procedure and its
complexity are supplied.

15. Counterexamples and obstruction claims

For every proposed counterexample, verify all hypotheses and the exact failure of the
conclusion. For every claimed impossibility or barrier theorem, verify that the class being
ruled out is formally defined and that the argument covers every method in that class.

16. Computational evidence

Check that finite experiments are used only for their stated purpose. Reject extrapolation
from tested cases unless a rigorous finite-reduction theorem justifies it.

1. a foundational audit focused on definitions, quantifiers, exceptional cases, and hidden

2. a domain-specialist audit focused on the central mathematical mechanism and the

3. a hostile counterexample audit whose sole objective is to break the strongest new lemma

4. [FOR ALGORITHMIC OR QUANTITATIVE RESULTS] a complexity and parameter audit focused
   on running time, encoding length, numerical precision, constants, approximation losses,

5. [INSERT ANY ADDITIONAL AUDIT ROLE PARTICULARLY IMPORTANT FOR THE PROBLEM].

If an audit discovers a gap, determine whether it is:

- a local omission with a valid proof already implicit in the established lemmas;

- a failure of a central invariant;

- a theorem-strength missing claim;

- a change to the target statement disguised as a repair.
